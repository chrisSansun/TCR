High Performance and Self-Doubt

Why Successful People Privately Doubt Themselves

By Christopher Murray · 8 minute read · Updated 20 June 2026

There's a particular kind of silence that shows up in my office. It belongs to people who, on paper, have nothing to doubt. They run companies, lead teams, win awards, and sit on the boards other people spend careers trying to reach. And they sit across from me describing a private conviction that none of it is quite real, that the achievement is somehow separate from them, borrowed rather than earned. This isn't rare. It's close to universal among the people I work with, and it's almost never about a lack of evidence.

Key Takeaways

  • Self-doubt in high achievers is rarely a competence problem. It's usually a gap between an outdated internal identity model and current external reality.
  • The brain works by minimising surprise. If success doesn't match the internal model of who you are, the nervous system treats the success as the anomaly, not the model.
  • The Solomon Paradox describes the documented gap between the wisdom people apply to others' problems and their inability to apply that same wisdom to themselves.
  • Closing the gap requires felt, embodied evidence over time, not more achievement and not better arguments.

Ravi was the chief medical officer of a health tech company, a published researcher respected well beyond his organisation. In board meetings, he spoke fast and buried himself in data, as though trying to outrun a question nobody had actually asked. "I know more about this subject than anyone in that room," he told me. "But the moment I stand up I feel like I'm defending a thesis, not leading a company." That single sentence captures the whole problem. His competence had grown. His internal sense of permission to occupy the room hadn't caught up.

The brain is a prediction machine, and yours might be running old data

Karl Friston's work on the brain describes it as a system constantly trying to minimise surprise. It builds an internal model of the world, including a model of who you are, and generates predictions from that model. When reality matches the prediction, the system stays calm. When reality deviates, stress rises, even when the deviation is good news. Confidence, in this frame, is simply a state where your internal model matches your external experience. You're not surprised by your own competence. You expected it.

Here's the uncomfortable part: when the model is outdated, the brain doesn't treat your current success as proof the model is wrong. It treats the success as the anomaly. You got lucky. The timing helped. They didn't look closely enough. The nervous system is protecting an identity it built years ago, and it will reinterpret almost any evidence to keep that identity intact, because changing the model feels riskier than living with the discomfort of doubt.

This is why reading another success-after-doubt story rarely helps for long, and why telling someone their fears are irrational rarely lands. You're offering new information to a system that isn't actually short on information. It's short on felt permission. That permission gets written early, often well before the achievements that should have overwritten it.

There's a cost to running this kind of prediction error constantly, separate from the discomfort of the doubt itself. A nervous system that's perpetually flagging your own success as an anomaly stays in a low-grade state of vigilance, scanning for the moment the mismatch gets corrected, the moment someone finally notices you don't belong at the level you're operating at. That vigilance is exhausting in a way that's hard to name, because from the outside everything looks fine. You're performing well, by every visible measure. Internally, a meaningful share of your energy is being spent managing a discrepancy nobody else can see, and that's before you've done any of the actual work in front of you.

The Solomon Paradox: wise about everyone except yourself

There's a name for part of this pattern. Psychologists Igor Grossmann and Ethan Kross documented what they called the Solomon Paradox: the gap between the wisdom we apply to other people's problems and our inability to apply that same wisdom to our own. You can see a friend's situation with total clarity, name the pattern, suggest the fix, and then go home and repeat the exact dynamic in your own life without recognising it.

I think about this constantly in my own work, because it doesn't disappear once you understand the mechanism. I can locate the architecture of someone else's problem in a single session and help them dismantle it, and I'll go home and catch myself in the same pattern I just helped a client see in themselves. For years I considered this a contradiction, even a disqualifying one. What changed my mind was realising it's not a failure of the framework. It's the framework working as intended. Every tool in this approach has to survive being used by someone who knows exactly how hard it is to live, not someone observing from a safe distance.

This matters for how you read your own doubt. The presence of doubt isn't evidence the work has failed. It's evidence you're human, applying a system that's genuinely harder to turn on yourself than on anyone else, which is precisely why it has to be practised rather than simply understood.

The empty win, and why achievement alone doesn't fill it

Amir, a property developer, had just completed the largest project of his career: a forty-million-pound mixed-use development, on time, on budget, glowing reviews from investors. He felt nothing. "I thought I would feel something," he told me. "Relief, pride, something. I just felt tired, and then guilty for feeling tired because I should have been celebrating."

This is what happens when confidence rests on conditional worth rather than a stable internal baseline. The pursuit itself generates dopamine and feels meaningful, because the nervous system is oriented toward a goal. The moment the goal lands, the fuel runs out, and there's no foundation underneath to land on, just a brief pause before the next target appears. Achievement, emptiness, pursuit, achievement, emptiness. The cycle doesn't resolve itself through more achievement, because more achievement is the mechanism that's failing to resolve it.

This is also why the relationship between competence and confidence is so often misread from the outside. People assume that if someone is visibly skilled, their internal experience must match. It rarely does. You can have high self-evaluation, what you'd call self-esteem, sitting alongside a low felt sense of safety in your own value. The head knows. The body hasn't agreed. Different protective patterns develop around this gap depending on what was rewarded and withheld early on, but the underlying split is the same one: knowing and feeling running on separate tracks.

I see this most clearly in the gap between how someone describes their achievements and how their body looks while doing it. Ask a high achiever to list what they've built and the words come fast, fluent, almost rehearsed. Ask them to sit with a compliment for ten seconds without deflecting it, and something different happens. The shoulders tighten slightly. The eyes drop. There's a small, almost imperceptible flinch, as though praise itself were a kind of exposure. That flinch is more honest than the list of achievements. It's the body telling you where the actual belief lives.

This is why deflecting praise so often gets misread as humility. It isn't. Genuine humility can sit still in the presence of a compliment. What looks like humility in many high achievers is actually discomfort, the nervous system trying to get out of a spotlight it has never fully trusted, even after years of standing in it successfully.

What actually closes the gap

If achievement doesn't close the gap, what does. The honest answer is felt, repeated, embodied evidence over time, not a single insight and not a better argument. The nervous system updates the way it learned in the first place: through experience that registers in the body, not just the mind.

Daniel, who we've discussed elsewhere in this work, didn't resolve thirty years of throat-tightening before investor pitches by being told he was competent. He'd heard that for thirty years. What shifted things was a different kind of rehearsal: naming the sensation before trying to manage it, and giving his nervous system new evidence, one pitch at a time, that visibility didn't carry the danger it once did. The mechanics of that regulation matter less than the consistency of the practice. The body needs repetition more than it needs explanation.

There's also a structural piece worth naming: staying inside your window of tolerance while you do this work matters. Pushing past it doesn't accelerate the update, it just teaches the nervous system that visibility is exhausting, which is its own kind of unhelpful evidence. The goal isn't to force the gap closed through willpower. It's to give the body small, survivable proofs, consistently enough, that the old model eventually loses its grip.

I don't think this gap ever fully disappears for most people, certainly not for me. What changes is your relationship to it. You stop treating the doubt as proof of fraud and start treating it as a lagging indicator, a part of you that's still updating. That reframe alone, paired with consistent practice, is usually where the shift actually begins.

Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

Is this the same as imposter syndrome?

It overlaps heavily. Imposter syndrome is usually the surface description, the feeling of being a fraud. What's described here is the underlying mechanism: an internal identity model that hasn't updated to match external reality, which produces that feeling.

Why doesn't reaching a bigger goal fix the doubt?

Because the nervous system isn't short on evidence of competence. It's short on felt permission to accept that evidence. A bigger goal just raises the bar the doubt measures against, the underlying gap stays the same size.

If I can see this pattern clearly in other people, why can't I see it in myself?

That's the Solomon Paradox in action, and it's well documented rather than a personal failing. Applying wisdom to your own situation is measurably harder than applying it to someone else's, for almost everyone, regardless of how self-aware they are in other areas.

How long does it typically take to feel a shift?

It varies by person and depth of the original pattern, but most people notice a change in baseline reactivity within three to four weeks of consistent daily practice. The doubt doesn't usually vanish. It becomes quieter and easier to recognise as outdated information rather than current truth.
