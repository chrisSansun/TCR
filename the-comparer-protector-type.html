The Comparer: Guarding Against Exclusion by Measuring Everyone Else

By Christopher Murray · 7 minute read · Updated 20 June 2026

Serena ran a digital marketing agency and was, by any measure, very good at what she did. Her clients stayed. Her team was loyal. Her revenue grew year on year. She also spent an hour every morning scrolling through often AI-enhanced LinkedIn profiles of people she considered her competitors, not researching, comparing, and walking away from that hour feeling like she was falling behind. That hour, every single morning, before she'd done any actual work, was the Comparer running at full volume.

Key Takeaways

  • The Comparer says "they're ahead of you," using constant measurement against others as a way of guarding against exclusion.
  • It develops in families or school environments where value was relative, where being good wasn't enough unless you were also ahead.
  • Social media has amplified this enormously, exposing people to curated highlights at a scale the social comparison circuitry never evolved to process.
  • The antidote isn't ignoring other people's success. It's returning to your own body and your own scorecard, deliberately and repeatedly.

Her critic's voice was specific: they're further ahead, they have better clients, they started later and they're already bigger. The voice was relentless, and loudest first thing in the morning, before the day had given her any actual evidence to argue with it. By the time she sat down to work, she'd already spent sixty minutes losing a competition nobody else knew she was running, against opponents who, in most cases, had no idea Serena existed at all.

How the Comparer forms: value that was always relative

The Comparer develops in families or school environments where value was relative rather than absolute, where being good at something was never quite enough on its own unless you were also ahead of someone specific. In our sessions, we traced Serena's pattern back to a family dynamic where her mother routinely held up other children as examples. Your cousin got into grammar school. The neighbour's daughter is doing so well. It was never delivered cruelly. It was aspirational, meant as motivation. But Serena's body absorbed something underneath the aspiration that had nothing to do with her mother's intent: you are not enough as you are, you need to keep up.

This is a useful distinction to hold onto, because the Comparer rarely forms through obvious cruelty. It usually forms through ordinary, well-meaning comparison, the kind most families use without a second thought, repeated often enough that a child's nervous system quietly concludes that worth is positional rather than inherent. Worth that depends on relative position is inherently unstable, because there's always someone ranked above you, which means the goalposts can never actually be reached, only chased.

What makes this protector particularly persistent is that it can be technically accurate in the moment while still being entirely unhelpful. Serena's competitors genuinely existed, and some of them genuinely were ahead by certain measures. The Comparer wasn't lying to her exactly. It was selectively curating a true but radically incomplete picture, the kind that omits her own client retention, her team's loyalty, her steady year-on-year growth, and presents only the gap, magnified, as though it were the entire story.

Why social media made this so much louder

Social media has amplified the Comparer to an extraordinary degree. You're now exposed to the curated highlights of thousands of people every single day, and the social comparison circuitry that evolved in small groups, roughly a hundred and fifty people, where you could see the full, unfiltered picture of someone's actual life, was never built for this volume. Now you see a fraction of a fraction, the best photo, the biggest win, the most flattering angle, and the nervous system, lacking better information, treats that fraction as the whole truth.

The result is a persistent, low-grade sense of inadequacy that has very little to do with your actual abilities. You're not falling short of some objective standard. You're comparing yourself to an illusion, a highlight reel assembled by someone with the same insecurities you have, and that comparison quietly drains the cognitive and emotional resources you actually need for your own work.

There's a particular cruelty to how this plays out for high achievers specifically, because the people most likely to be visible online, the ones whose updates trigger comparison in the first place, are disproportionately people who are themselves performing confidence rather than feeling it. Two anxious, accomplished people can spend a morning each quietly envying a curated version of the other, both convinced the other has it figured out, neither aware that the comparison is running in both directions simultaneously, fuelled by images neither of them would recognise as their own internal experience.

Serena's shift: from scrolling to building

We worked with Serena's Comparer the same way I work with every protector type: not by trying to silence it, but by thanking it directly. Thank you for trying to keep me motivated, I can see what you're doing, but I'm going to check my own scorecard today, not theirs. It sounds almost too simple written down, and the simplicity is part of why it works. The Comparer doesn't need to be defeated in an argument. It needs to be acknowledged and gently redirected toward a different question.

Serena started her mornings differently. Instead of scrolling, she'd write one sentence about something she'd built that she was proud of, not something she'd achieved relative to anyone else, just built. The distinction mattered more than it might seem, because achievement implies comparison by its very structure, while building implies creation, something that exists on its own terms regardless of what anyone else has done. Her body responded to the shift within days, not because the competitors disappeared, but because she'd stopped handing them the first hour of every morning.

What made this particular substitution effective, rather than just another productivity hack, was that it addressed the actual mechanism rather than the symptom. Telling Serena to simply stop comparing herself to others would have been advice without a foothold, since the impulse wasn't a choice she was making consciously, it was a habit her nervous system had been running on autopilot for years. Giving her body something concrete to do instead, one sentence, one act of noticing what already existed rather than what was missing, gave the old pattern somewhere else to go.

The antidote: returning to your own body

The antidote to comparison isn't ignoring other people's success, which tends to be both unrealistic and unnecessary. It's returning to your own body when the comparison impulse arrives. Notice where it lands physically, the chest, the stomach, the throat, then exhale long and ask what quality in that person you admire that already exists in you. This turns the comparison from a threat into a recognition. The brain shifts from separation toward resonance, and resonance, unlike comparison, doesn't cost you anything to feel, which makes it one of the rare interventions that actually replenishes energy rather than draining it.

I think the Comparer is one of the more exhausting protectors to carry precisely because the comparison never resolves. There's always someone further along, by some metric, in some direction, which means a strategy built on outranking others can never actually deliver the safety it promises. The relief doesn't come from winning the comparison. It comes from stepping out of the game entirely, even briefly, long enough to notice what you've actually built on your own terms.

What I find most encouraging about cases like Serena's is how quickly the body responds once the comparison loop actually loosens, even slightly. She didn't need months of work before noticing a difference. A single redirected morning, replacing the scroll with one honest sentence about what she'd built, produced a felt shift within days. That speed isn't unusual. The Comparer, for all its persistence, tends to be remarkably responsive to even small, consistent interruptions, because the pattern depends on repetition to maintain its grip, and breaking the repetition, even imperfectly, starts loosening it almost immediately.

Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

Is the Comparer the same thing as ambition?

They're related but distinct. Ambition is typically goal-directed and can coexist with genuine satisfaction. The Comparer is specifically anxiety-driven, oriented around relative position rather than absolute progress, which means it rarely produces lasting satisfaction even when the comparison currently looks favourable.

Why does the Comparer often get worse on social media specifically?

Because social media presents curated highlights at a volume and frequency the social comparison circuitry never evolved to process. You're comparing your full, unfiltered life to other people's best moments, repeated dozens of times a day, which skews the comparison badly in one consistent direction.

Can the Comparer coexist with genuine confidence in other areas?

Yes, quite commonly. Someone can feel entirely secure in their technical skill while the Comparer runs loudly around a specific domain, like visibility, income, or social standing. The pattern tends to be domain-specific rather than a uniform trait across someone's entire identity.

Does limiting social media use actually help with the Comparer?

It can reduce the frequency of triggers, but it doesn't address the underlying pattern on its own. The deeper work involves changing the relationship to comparison itself, so that even occasional exposure to someone else's highlight reel doesn't automatically trigger the full response.
